To assess robustness beyond the single-run baseline, we conduct a
velocity-sweep experiment over four maximum progress speeds
$v_{\theta,\max} \in \{4.74,\,5.32,\,6.1,\,7.5\}\;\si{m/s}$.
For each speed, 50 Monte Carlo trials are executed with randomly
perturbed initial conditions
($\sigma_p = \SI{0.05}{m}$, $\sigma_q = \SI{0.05}{rad}$)
on the same \SI{100}{m} Lissajous path; all other parameters
(cost weights, constraints, prediction horizon, control frequency)
remain identical to Experiment~I.
This design isolates the effect of the state representation under
increasing dynamic demand: higher $v_{\theta,\max}$ forces the solver
to plan faster motions, amplifying any shortcomings in the pose
error model.
Fig.~\ref{fig:velocity_sweep} reports the distribution of
position and orientation RMSE across the 50 runs, and
Table~\ref{tab:velocity_sweep} collects the corresponding
median values with interquartile ranges.

At the lowest speed ($v_{\theta,\max} = \SI{4.74}{m/s}$), both
controllers achieve comparable position tracking, consistent with
Experiment~I: the $\mathrm{SE}(3)$ representation offers marginal gains
when the trajectory is traversed slowly.
As $v_{\theta,\max}$ increases, however, the advantage of DQ-MPCC
becomes increasingly pronounced.
The median position RMSE of DQ-MPCC grows gracefully with speed, while
the baseline exhibits a steeper degradation and wider interquartile
spread, reflecting greater sensitivity to the perturbed initial
conditions.
A similar trend is observed in the orientation metric: the coupled
rotation–translation cost of the dual-quaternion logarithm maintains
tighter rotational tracking at high speeds, whereas the decoupled
quaternion error of the baseline accrues larger attitude residuals
once the dynamic demand exceeds a threshold near
$v_{\theta,\max} \approx \SI{6}{m/s}$.
Above this threshold, the baseline also begins to present solver
failures, highlighted in Fig.~\ref{fig:velocity_sweep};
DQ-MPCC records no failures across the entire sweep.
These results demonstrate that the $\mathrm{SE}(3)$ formulation not only
improves steady-state tracking (Experiment~I) but also scales more
robustly to aggressive manoeuvers, making it the preferred
representation for MPCC-based quadrotor control in high-speed regimes.
